# Skill: Proposal writing

## Goal

Convince a decision-maker to say "yes" — to a budget, a project, an engagement, or a partnership. The proposal answers four questions, in order: *Why now? What are we proposing? What will it cost? How will we know it worked?*

## Structure

```
# Proposal: <Concrete title — what we're proposing, not the category>

**Prepared by:** <names> · **For:** <decision-maker(s)> · **Date:** <YYYY-MM-DD>

## The opportunity (or: the problem)
<3-5 sentences. The thing that's true RIGHT NOW that creates the opening — a market shift, a customer pain, a competitor move, a deadline. Quantify it. Without an opportunity, there's no proposal.>

## What we're proposing
<2-3 sentences. The plain-English version. If the decider only reads this paragraph, what should they walk away with?>

## How we'll do it
<3-7 bullets or short paragraphs. The approach — concrete enough that the decider believes you can actually execute, not so detailed that you've already done the work for free.>

## What it costs
| Item | Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| <People — N FTEs for M weeks> | $X | <skill mix> |
| <Tools / licenses> | $X | |
| <Other> | $X | |
| **Total** | **$X** | <timeline summary> |

## What success looks like
- <Metric 1 — current value → target value — measured how / by when>
- <Metric 2>
- <Leading indicator we'd see in the first 30 days>

## Risks & mitigations
- **<Top risk>** — <how we'd mitigate or how we'd know to abort>
- <Second risk>

## Why us / why now
<One paragraph. Why this team, why this approach, why this timing beats waiting. Optional but powerful for external proposals.>

## Decision needed
<The literal ask. "We're seeking approval to start by <date> with a budget of <$X>." No ambiguity.>
```

## Rules

- **Lead with the opportunity, not yourselves.** Decision-makers care about their problem; your credentials come at the end.
- **Quantify everything that can be quantified.** "Save time" → "save the support team 12 hours/week".
- **Show the cost, don't hide it.** A proposal without a clearly-stated cost section gets reflexively rejected.
- **Success metrics are required.** A proposal that can't define "won" is asking for a blank check.
- **One ask per proposal.** Two intertwined asks become "let's discuss" — which means no.
- **Acknowledge what could go wrong.** Decision-makers trust proposers who name risks.

## Length

External / large budget: 800-1500 words.
Internal / smaller scope: 400-800 words.
If you can't make the case in 1500 words, the case has gaps — fix the case, not the length.

## Pitfalls

- Pages of company history before the proposal — bury that in an appendix.
- Vague success metrics ("improve customer satisfaction") with no baseline or target.
- Asking for "buy-in" instead of a specific approval. Buy-in is a wish; approval is a budget line.
- No timeline. "We could start once approved" tells the decider you haven't planned.
