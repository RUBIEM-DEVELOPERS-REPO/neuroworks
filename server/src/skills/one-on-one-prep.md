# Skill: 1:1 prep

## Goal

A 1:1 is the most leveraged 30 minutes on either calendar. Prep makes the difference between "we had a chat" and "we resolved something specific". Different roles, different prep.

## If you are the manager

### Before the meeting

Review:
- Their previous 1:1 notes — what was open, what was committed
- Their work output in the last week (PRs / docs / shipped artifacts)
- Anything from skip-levels, peers, or stakeholders that's relevant
- Any feedback you've been sitting on — overdue feedback should land in 1:1, not in performance review

### Draft an agenda (their items first, yours second)

```
- (their items — they lead this section)
- (specific feedback I owe them today: <thing> — frame: situation + impact + ask)
- (project / decision check-in: <thing>)
- (career / growth thread: <follow-up from last cycle>)
- (org context I can share: <thing>)
```

### Questions a manager should keep in rotation

- "What's blocking you right now that I could move?"
- "Where do you feel I'm not giving you enough context / direction / autonomy?"
- "What part of the work is energising? Draining?"
- "If you were running this team, what would you change?"
- "What do you want to learn that you're not learning right now?"

Don't ask all of these every week — pick one that suits the moment.

## If you are the report

### Before the meeting

Walk in with:
- 2-3 things you want to raise (don't make them guess)
- A specific ask (decision needed / unblocker / feedback you want)
- Updates on prior commitments — what you did, what slipped

```
## My agenda for this 1:1

1. <Decision I need from you on X by <date>>
2. <Update: shipped Y, blocked on Z, here's how I'd like to handle it>
3. <Feedback I want: how did the X presentation land?>
4. <Career thread: I'd like to take on more <type of work> — here's why>
```

### Things to bring up in a 1:1 (not in chat)

- Anything that's been weighing on you for more than a few days
- Feedback you want from them (not just feedback they have for you)
- Conflicts with peers — bring the conflict, not the resolution
- Career / growth — these always lose to "urgent" stuff if you don't carve out time
- Anything you'd say in resignation that you haven't said before — say it 6 months earlier

## Output shape (when generating a prep doc for the customer)

```
# 1:1 prep with <other person> — <date>

## Their context (recent)
- <what they've been doing>
- <what they raised last time>
- <any open commitments>

## Topics I want to cover
1. <Topic — desired outcome — 1-2 questions to ask>
2. <Topic — desired outcome>
3. <Topic — desired outcome>

## Feedback to deliver
- <Specific moment + impact + ask. Not vague: "you handled the X meeting well — specifically, you de-escalated when Y pushed back. Keep doing that.">

## Questions to listen for
- <Open thread from last time>
- <Their energy / load — am I overloading them or underloading them?>

## What I should NOT use the time for
- <Things that should be email/async — keep the 1:1 for things that need conversation>
```

## Rules

- **Their topics first.** A 1:1 the manager dominates is a status meeting in disguise.
- **Write things down between meetings.** A 1:1 you prep for in the elevator is a worse 1:1.
- **Don't cancel.** Reschedule, never cancel. The signal is louder than the lost half hour.
- **End with explicit follow-ups.** Both sides should know who's doing what by when.
- **Async first, sync for the rest.** Use the time for what can't happen over chat: feedback, career, conflict, nuance.

## Pitfalls

- Status-update 1:1s — should be an async doc instead, freeing the time for real talk.
- Manager talking 70%+ of the time.
- Skipping career and growth threads because there's always something more urgent.
- Reviewing a long meta-list every time — pick 2-3 substantive things and go deep.
- Never giving negative feedback in 1:1, saving it for performance review — that's how surprises happen.
