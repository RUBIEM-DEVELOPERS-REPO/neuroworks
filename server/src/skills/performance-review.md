# Skill: Performance review

## Goal

A review the recipient finds *fair* — they don't need to agree with every line, but the picture should match the year as they lived it. Vague reviews destroy trust; specific reviews build it even when they include criticism.

## Universal rules

- **Evidence-based.** Every claim — positive or critical — points to a specific moment, project, or outcome. No "great team player" without an example.
- **Whole year, not last month.** A review skewed toward recent events is biased. Pull notes from across all four quarters.
- **Behaviors, not personality.** "Pushed back constructively on the Q3 plan" beats "good attitude". One is actionable; the other is character.
- **Concrete development direction.** A review without a "what to work on next" section is incomplete.
- **Calibrated.** Compare to the level expectations for the role, not to the strongest peer on the team.

## Manager-written review

```
# Performance Review — <Name> — <Period>

## Summary
<3-5 sentences. The headline rating + the 2-3 most important things that drove it. Lead with the strongest signal — positive if positive, mixed if mixed.>

## Impact this period

### What you shipped
- <Project — your role — outcome / metric>
- <Project — your role — outcome / metric>

### Where you raised the bar
- <Specific change you drove that improved how the team / org operates. With one example.>

## Strengths
- **<Strength — short label>**: <Specific instance. Why it matters. What it means for what you should do more of.>
- **<Strength>**: <...>

## Areas to develop
- **<Area — short label>**: <Specific moment(s) that surfaced this. The pattern. The concrete next step — a behavior change, a stretch project, a skill to build.>
- **<Area>**: <...>

## Calibration notes
<Comparison to role / level expectations. Where you exceeded, met, or missed.>

## Next period — focus
- <Top 2-3 things to work on. Not 7. Specific enough that a year from now we can both tell if it happened.>

## Rating *(if your org uses one)*
<Rating + 1-2 sentence rationale.>
```

## Self-review (writing about yourself)

```
# Self-Review — <Name> — <Period>

## What I shipped
- <Project — my role — outcome — link or evidence>
- <Project>

## What I'm proud of
- <Specific moment + why it mattered>
- <Decision you made that you'd make the same way again>

## What I'd do differently
- <Specific moment + what you learned + what you'd change>
- <Be honest. A self-review with no real "I'd do differently" reads as low-self-awareness.>

## How I've grown
- <Skill or perspective — what enabled the growth — evidence>

## Where I want to grow next
- <Capability or scope — why now — what support I'd want>

## Feedback for my manager / team
- <One thing that would help me work better>
- <One thing the team / org could improve>
```

## Rules for writing about specific moments

Use the **STAR** shape inline:
- **Situation:** what was the context
- **Task:** what was at stake
- **Action:** what they (or you) specifically did
- **Result:** what happened because of it

You don't need to label STAR — just include all four. "When the Q3 launch hit a regression on day 2, Sam pushed back on the rollback plan, suggested we ship a hotfix instead, ran it through review in 90 minutes, and got us back to baseline before the next ad spend hit."

## Pitfalls

- "Strong communicator / collaborator / team player" — vague. Replace with the moment they collaborated well.
- "Could improve communication" — not actionable. Replace with "spent Q3 over-relying on async — invite live conversation when scope is unclear, e.g. the X situation".
- Recency bias — heavy weight on the last 6 weeks. Pull notes from Q1-Q2 specifically.
- Halo / horn effect — one big success / failure dominating everything. Force yourself to find evidence outside the headline event.
- Surprises in a review that should have been raised earlier — they damage trust regardless of accuracy.
- "Meets expectations" with nothing to develop — that itself is feedback. Either it's a stretch year ahead, or you're missing signal.
