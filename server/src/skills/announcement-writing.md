# Skill: Announcement writing

## Goal

An announcement is read by people in mixed contexts, on mixed devices, in mixed levels of attention. It has to land in the first paragraph for the skimmers and survive scrutiny from the readers who read the whole thing.

## Format

```
# <Headline — the news itself, in one specific sentence>

**TL;DR:** <One sentence — the thing the reader should walk away with. Same as the headline but with the key context.>

## What's happening
<2-4 sentences. The change, in plain English. No jargon. No corporate-speak. State the FACT before any spin.>

## Why
<2-3 sentences. The reasoning. Honest, not promotional. Readers detect spin instantly and trust the rest of the announcement less.>

## What this means for you / your team
<Concrete impact. By audience if it differs. "If you're in <group>, here's what changes for you on <date>." If nothing changes for someone, say that too — silence reads as "they didn't think about us".>

## Timeline
- <YYYY-MM-DD: <event>>
- <YYYY-MM-DD: <event>>
- <YYYY-MM-DD: <event>>

## What's NOT changing
<Sometimes more important than what is. Heads off anxiety, kills rumours.>

## Where to ask questions
<Channel, person, AMA time. People will ask anyway — funnel it.>

— <Author / role>
```

## Variants by topic

### Launch announcement
- Lead with what's now possible (user benefit), not what's now built (feature list)
- Include a demo / screenshot / quickstart link in the first 200 words
- Name who's responsible for what after launch — support, on-call, feedback

### Hire announcement
- Name + role + start date in the headline
- 1-2 sentences on why this hire matters strategically (not just bio)
- Bio second, in 3-5 sentences max
- "How to reach them" / first week onboarding instructions

### Departure announcement
- Be honest without oversharing. "X is leaving to <pursue Y / spend more time with Z>" if they're comfortable being specific; otherwise "X is leaving to take a new opportunity".
- Thank them sincerely, specifically (one or two real things they did).
- State what happens to their work — who's the interim owner, when's the transition plan.
- Last day. How colleagues can stay in touch if appropriate.

### Policy / process change
- State the change FIRST, the rationale SECOND. Most people scan; bury the change in the rationale and they think nothing changed.
- Effective date in the headline if it's near-term.
- What changes for whom — by team, by role, by employment status (FTE / contractor / customer).
- Grandfathering rules — explicit. Ambiguity is more expensive than slightly broader generosity.
- Who to talk to if there's a hardship case.

### Strategic pivot / restructure
- Lead with what's true, not aspirational. "We're consolidating from 4 product lines to 2" → say which two and which two are sunsetting.
- Acknowledge what people will feel before you frame why it's right.
- People > products in the first half of the announcement (impact on roles, transitions, support); business case in the second half.

## Rules

- **No marketing-speak in internal announcements.** "Synergize", "leverage", "best-in-class" → cut.
- **Specificity beats reassurance.** "Your team is unaffected" beats "We're committed to supporting all our teams during this transition".
- **Don't bury the lead.** If the headline could apply to 10 different announcements, it's too vague.
- **One announcement, one topic.** Bundling a launch with a layoff makes both worse.
- **Read it as the most affected person.** Then rewrite.

## Length

Most announcements: 200-500 words.
Major announcements (restructure, big launch): 500-900 words.
If you're past 900 words, you have a memo with an announcement headline — split.

## Pitfalls

- Sandwiching bad news between two pieces of good news ("shit sandwich") — readers see it and trust drops.
- Hedging language that fooled nobody ("we may need to consider...").
- Posting at 5pm Friday for hard news — readers learn to dread your byline.
- Comments / replies disabled with no alternative channel — looks like a verdict, not a conversation.
- Forgetting "where to ask questions" — every announcement creates them.
