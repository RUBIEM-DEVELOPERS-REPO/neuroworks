# Skill: 1-page brief

## Goal

A single-screen doc that gives a decision-maker enough to say yes / no / "ask me again on X" without reading anything else.

## Structure

```
**<Title — what this brief is asking for>**
_<1-line context: who / when / why this exists>_

## TL;DR
<2-3 sentences. The recommendation + the single most important reason.>

## Context
<3-5 bullets — the facts that motivate this. What changed? What's at stake?>

## Options
<Each option named + one-sentence summary + key trade-off>
1. **Option A — <name>:** <what it is> · Pro: <…> · Con: <…>
2. **Option B — <name>:** <…>
3. **Option C — <name>:** <…>

## Recommendation
<Which one, in one sentence. Then 2-3 sentences on why.>

## Open questions
- <thing the decision-maker needs to weigh in on, or info still missing>
```

## Rules

- **One screen.** If it scrolls past one laptop screen, it's not a brief — it's a doc.
- **Recommendation, not just options.** A brief that ends "you decide" wastes the reader's time. Pick one, defend it, name what would change your mind.
- **Bold the bottom line.** The TL;DR + Recommendation should survive a 10-second skim.
- **Numbers where you can.** "Saves 40 hrs/quarter" beats "saves significant time".
- **Open questions, not "let me know if questions".** Be specific about what's unresolved.

## Pitfalls

- Burying the recommendation at the end ("After consideration of various factors…") — put it on screen-line one.
- Listing 7 options. 3 is the cap; 2 is often better.
- Hedging the recommendation ("we might consider potentially…") — commit or punt explicitly.
- Adding an "Executive summary" section in addition to TL;DR — the TL;DR IS the executive summary.
