# Skill: Feedback giving

## Goal

Feedback that the recipient hears clearly, can act on, and doesn't have to interpret. The cost of giving feedback poorly is silent: the person doesn't change, you stop trusting them, the relationship erodes invisibly.

## The model: Situation → Behavior → Impact → Ask (SBI-A)

```
- **Situation:** when and where (specific moment, not "in general")
- **Behavior:** what you observed (action, not character)
- **Impact:** the consequence — for the work, the team, the person
- **Ask:** the specific change you'd like, OR the specific thing to keep doing
```

### Example — corrective

> "In yesterday's design review (S), when Priya asked about the latency budget, you cut her off twice before she finished the question (B). It looked like you'd decided the answer before hearing her — I noticed her stop engaging for the rest of the meeting (I). Next time, can you let questions land fully before responding? Especially when it's someone newer to the room (A)."

### Example — reinforcing (positive feedback that's actually useful)

Vague: "Great job in the review."
Useful:

> "In Tuesday's design review (S), when Sam pushed back on the rollback plan, you stayed calm, asked two clarifying questions, then proposed the hotfix approach (B). The room shifted from defensive to collaborative immediately (I). Keep doing that — it's a thing only a few people on this team do well (A — implicit "keep going")."

## When to give feedback

- **As close to the event as possible.** Feedback delivered 3 weeks later is half-effective. Same-day or next-day for behavioral feedback.
- **In private for corrective; in public is OK for reinforcing** (some people prefer private even for positive — read the room).
- **Not when either of you is in fight-or-flight.** A 1-hour cooling period helps; a 1-week wait erodes the value.

## Hard feedback

For feedback that's likely to land hard:

1. **Name your intent up front.** "I want to share something I think might be hard to hear, because I want us to keep working well together."
2. **State the SBI-A.** Don't pad it with throat-clearing.
3. **Pause for response.** Don't fill the silence — they need a beat.
4. **Acknowledge what's true in their view.** Even if you don't agree, name the part you do.
5. **End with a forward-looking ask.** Not "don't do X" but "next time, try Y".

## When to NOT give feedback

- It's about character, not behavior. "You're not strategic" is unactionable.
- You don't have a specific situation in mind.
- You're giving it because you're frustrated, not because the recipient can act on it. (Cool off first.)
- It's stylistic preference, not impact. "I'd write that email differently" — keep it.

## Output shape (when generating feedback for the customer to deliver)

```
# Feedback for <name> — <topic>

**Context I'm using:** <what you, the assistant, know about the situation>

**Suggested delivery:**

"<Situation — specific moment + setting>. When <behavior — what they did, observable>, <impact — consequence, with one piece of evidence>. <Ask — specific, forward-looking>."

**Things to be ready for:**
- They might respond with <likely reaction> — here's how to acknowledge while holding the point.
- They might disagree about <specific claim> — here's how to handle.

**Don't say:**
- <Common phrasings that turn this into character feedback or vague hedging>
```

## Rules

- **Behavior, not character.** "You came across as dismissive" → "You interrupted Priya twice".
- **One thing per conversation.** A list of 5 critiques is a performance review, not feedback.
- **No "shit sandwich".** Sandwiching corrective feedback between two compliments is widely recognised — recipients tune out the corrective part. Be direct + kind, not indirect.
- **Specific evidence.** Vague feedback creates anxiety without direction.
- **Forward-looking ask.** Without the "A", you've described a problem without owning a solution.

## How to ASK for feedback (the reverse skill)

- Ask specifically. "Any feedback?" gets you nothing. "How did the way I handled the X part of the review land?" gets you signal.
- Ask within 24 hours of the event. Memory degrades.
- Don't defend in the moment. Capture, thank, then process later.
- Follow up on what you tried in response. Closes the loop, signals you take it seriously.

## Pitfalls

- "I don't want to make a big deal of it but…" → already a big deal. Just say it.
- Giving the feedback to their manager instead of them. (Sometimes appropriate; usually a cop-out.)
- "People are saying…" — own the feedback yourself, or attribute it accurately.
- Asking "do you have feedback?" without making space (deflecting answers with "great point, but…").
- Skipping positive feedback entirely. Reinforcement is half the system.
