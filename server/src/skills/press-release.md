# Skill: Press release

## Goal

A journalist can lift the first two paragraphs into a story with zero edits, and a reader gets the news in the headline + first sentence. External-facing, third person, no internal jargon.

## Structure

```
FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE

# <Headline: the news in <12 words, active voice>
### <Subhead: the so-what / second-most-important fact>

**<CITY, Country — <Month Day, Year>** — <Lede: who did what, when, why it matters — one sentence, inverted pyramid.>

<Paragraph 2: the substance — the most important supporting facts, numbers, scope.>

"<Quote from a named leader — says something a human would actually say, not marketing filler>," said <Name>, <Title> at <Org>. "<Second sentence adds vision or customer impact.>"

<Paragraph 3: context — why now, market backdrop, what's next, availability/dates.>

<Optional: customer or partner quote.>

### About <Org>
<2-3 sentence boilerplate — what the company does, founded, footprint.>

### Media contact
<Name> · <email> · <phone>

###
```

## Rules

- **News in the headline.** If the reader only sees the headline, they should know what happened.
- **Inverted pyramid.** Most important fact first; details decay downward. A sub can cut from the bottom and it still reads.
- **Quotes sound human.** No "We are thrilled to leverage synergies." A quote should carry an opinion or vision a person would say aloud.
- **Third person, external voice.** Not "we shipped" — "<Org> launched".
- **Every claim is checkable** — dates, numbers, names. No superlatives without proof ("first", "leading" only if defensible).
- End with `###` (the standard "end of release" mark).

## Pitfalls

- Burying the news under company history — lede first, boilerplate last.
- Hype adjectives (revolutionary, game-changing, cutting-edge) — journalists delete them; they signal no real news.
- A quote that just restates the headline — waste the most quotable line.
- Missing the dateline, contact, or boilerplate — looks unfinished to a desk editor.
- Confusing with an INTERNAL announcement (that's the announcement-writing skill — different audience and tone).
