# Skill: Objection handling

## Goal

The rep walks into the meeting having pre-thought the likely objections.
For each one, they have a calibrated response that ACKNOWLEDGES the
objection (not dismisses it) and REFRAMES toward a better question.

## The pattern (every response uses it)

```
1. Acknowledge   — "That's a fair concern."
2. Reframe       — "Most customers we talk to ask the same thing, and
                    what they end up looking at is <reframe>."
3. Specific      — Concrete example, customer story, or number.
4. Forward       — "Would it help if we showed you <next step>?"
```

NEVER skip #1. Jumping straight to reframe reads dismissive.

## Library — common objections

### "Too expensive"
- **Acknowledge:** That's a fair check.
- **Reframe:** Most teams that compare us on price end up comparing total
  cost — implementation, hidden seats, support hours.
- **Specific:** Customer X switched from a cheaper tool and saved $40K/yr
  in implementation time.
- **Forward:** Want me to send a side-by-side total-cost breakdown vs
  <competitor>?

### "We have no budget"
- **Acknowledge:** Understood — and you're not alone, most teams find
  budget late in the year.
- **Reframe:** The question I'd ask is whether the problem we discussed
  is something you'd find budget for in the next budget cycle.
- **Specific:** If yes, here's what's worked — we lock in pricing now and
  start in Q<X>.
- **Forward:** Want me to send you a quote you could bring to finance for
  next-cycle planning?

### "We already have X"
- **Acknowledge:** Makes sense — most of our customers had a tool before
  switching.
- **Reframe:** The pattern we see is teams use X for <its strength> and
  add us for <our specific edge>.
- **Specific:** <Customer> still uses X and uses us for <X-specific
  workflow>.
- **Forward:** Worth a 30-min compatibility chat? I won't try to convince
  you to switch — just show where they coexist.

### "Not the right time"
- **Acknowledge:** Totally fair, timing matters.
- **Reframe:** Help me understand the WHY — is it priorities, is it
  capacity, or is it that the pain isn't acute enough yet?
- **Specific:** Most "not now" answers we hear are either capacity or
  priorities — both have specific paths forward.
- **Forward:** Can we book a check-in for <specific date> so I'm not
  chasing you, but we don't lose the thread?

### "Send me more info"
- **Acknowledge:** Will do.
- **Reframe:** To send you the right thing — what's the one question you
  most need answered?
- **Specific:** I can send a one-pager, a security overview, a case
  study, or a calculator — pick one.
- **Forward:** I'll send it today. Want a call to walk through it next
  week?

### "I need to talk to my team"
- **Acknowledge:** Of course.
- **Reframe:** What would help the conversation — should I prep a
  one-pager you can share, or join the meeting?
- **Specific:** Most customers find it easier when we're in the room for
  the technical question.
- **Forward:** Want me to send a calendar slot for next week?

## Process for a NEW objection

When the rep hits an objection not in the library:

1. **Don't react.** Repeat back: "So if I'm hearing you right, you're
   saying…"
2. **Confirm.** Get a yes before responding.
3. **Apply the pattern.** Acknowledge → Reframe → Specific → Forward.
4. **Log it** — this objection joins the library for next time.

## Rules

- **Never argue.** "Actually, you're wrong about X" loses the deal even
  when you're right.
- **Never panic-discount.** "Let me get you a 20% off" turns a real
  conversation into a transaction.
- **Always end with a forward.** Without a next step, the objection wins.

## Pitfalls

- Reframing too aggressively. "But our customers find…" too early reads
  defensive.
- Discounting on first push. Establishes that price is negotiable on
  every future contract.
- Treating "I need to think" as a real objection — it's usually a
  proxy for unspoken concern. Probe.
